The real problem

It's not that your teams
don't use AI.

They do. Individually, inconsistently, and often without understanding what happens to the data they feed into external tools. The risk isn't that AI is ignored : it is used without guardrails.

At the same time, the efficiency gains that AI should deliver , hours reclaimed from repetitive drafting, reporting, consolidation, aren't materialising. Not because the tools don't work. Because the workflows haven't been restructured to use them properly.

What this looks like in practice

·

Beneficiary data entered into ChatGPT because someone needed a quick translation and didn't think it through.

·

Five staff writing the same sitrep five different ways. None using the prompt structure that would cut the time in half.

·

AI outputs treated as final : hallucinations in donor reports, incorrect figures in logistics summaries, no verification step.

·

No shared language between departments. Finance uses one tool, programme another, coordination none. Zero cross-team learning.

·

Zero institutional memory. When the person who figured it out leaves, the knowledge leaves with them.

What this programme produces

Not a training.
A system.

Most AI trainings produce awareness. This programme produces infrastructure: the shared language, the department-specific toolkits, and the institutional protocols that make AI use consistent, safe and measurable.

01

A compliance baseline every department operates from

Two all-staff foundation sessions define what AI can and cannot receive from your organisation: beneficiary data, health records, financial information, staff data. Everyone leaves with a department-level data protocol they drafted themselves. Not a policy from HQ. A working protocol, built during the session.

02

Six department-specific prompt libraries

One module per department: Finance, Logistics, Procurement, Operations, Coordination, Technical. Content defined by a 30-minute pre-session scoping with each department head. Every prompt tested against your actual documents during the session. The library produced is yours at handover.

03

An AI reference guide your organisation actually uses

The closing session consolidates everything built across ten weeks : what works, what does not, which practices to sustain, recommendations for a second cycle. Formatted for onboarding new staff. Not for a shelf.

Structure

Ten weeks.
Three layers.

The sequence is deliberate. Compliance before productivity. Foundation before specialisation. Shared language before department-specific toolkits.

Week 1
Pre-programme scoping
30-minute intake with each department head. Pain points, highest-volume tasks, existing tool usage, data sensitivity profile. This defines session content. Not a generic curriculum.
Wk 2–3
Foundation curriculum, all staff
Session 1: AI landscape, ethics, bias, hallucination risks, what changes in daily workflows and what doesn't. Session 2: data protection, GDPR obligations, what goes into an AI tool and what never does. Every participant leaves with a draft department data protocol.
Wk 4–9
Specialised modules, one per department
Finance, Logistics, Procurement, Operations & Programme, Coordination, Technical. Each session targets the specific high-volume tasks identified in pre-module scoping. Every module produces one shared prompt library, tested against real department files.
Week 10
Closing session
Cross-team lessons learned, updated AI reference guide, recommendations for next steps. No new tools introduced. The session produces the institutional memory the organisation needs to sustain adoption independently.
